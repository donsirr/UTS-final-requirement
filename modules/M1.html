Who is the person that has inspired you to take your current course or significantly influenced your life choices? Share a specific story or moment that demonstrates their influence on you. How do their characteristics, whether Eastern or Western, shape their personality and interactions with others? Compare these traits to your own.
The person who inspired me to take my current course which is Computer Science is my father. Reflect on your daily habits, traditions, or beliefs. Identify one or two specific practices that you think are influenced by Eastern or Western culture. How do these practices reflect your identity? Have you ever struggled to reconcile these influences? Share a personal experience that illustrates this.
One daily habit influenced by Eastern culture is my practice of journaling and reflection, which I adopted after learning about the Japanese concept of "Ikigai" (a reason for being) and the importance of self-awareness in many Eastern philosophies. Every evening, I spend 10–15 minutes writing about my day, my emotions, and my goals. This practice helps me stay grounded and mindful, aligning with Eastern values of introspection and harmony. On the other hand, a Western-influenced habit is my focus on goal-setting and productivity, inspired by the Western emphasis on individual achievement and self-improvement. I use planners and apps to track tasks, a practice that reflects my ambition and drive to succeed in a competitive academic environment.

These practices reflect my identity as someone who values both inner peace and external accomplishment. The Eastern-inspired journaling helps me connect with my emotional and spiritual self, while the Western-influenced productivity habits fuel my desire to grow and achieve. However, I’ve often struggled to reconcile these influences. For example, during my senior high school days, I became so focused on achieving "With Honors" that I neglected my mental health and relationships. I stopped journaling, feeling it was a "waste of time" compared to studying. This led to burnout and a sense of emptiness, as if I was losing touch with my core self.

A turning point came during a conversation with my mother, who grew up in a traditional Filipino household. She noticed my stress and reminded me somewhat similar to what a Confucian would say: "The superior man is modest in his speech but exceeds in his actions." She encouraged me to reconnect with my journaling practice, emphasizing that true success comes from balance, not just achievement. I took her advice and restarted my reflection habit, which helped me regain perspective and prioritize self-care alongside my goals. This experience taught me that my identity is not about choosing between Eastern or Western influences but about integrating them in a way that honors both my roots and my aspirations.
Think about a situation where you encountered cultural differences, either in school, at home, or in your community. How did you respond to those differences, and what did you learn? Based on this experience, how can you use your role as a student or future professional to promote understanding and empathy? Provide at least one concrete action you can take within your field.
During my senior year in high school, I encountered cultural differences in a group project with classmates from diverse backgrounds. One team member, a student from a collectivist culture, prioritized group harmony and often avoided voicing disagreements during discussions. In contrast, another member, from an individualist culture, was direct and assertive, sometimes coming across as dismissive of others’ ideas. Initially, I felt frustrated by the lack of "progress" in our meetings, as I was favored to a more direct, Western-style approach to problem-solving.

My response evolved over time. At first, I tried to push the group to make quicker decisions, which only heightened tensions. However, after observing the dynamics, I realized that the collectivist team member had valuable insights but needed a safe space to express them. I began to facilitate discussions by explicitly inviting everyone to share their thoughts and summarizing points to ensure all voices were heard. This approach helped bridge the gap between the two perspectives and allowed us to complete the project successfully.

From this experience, I learned that cultural differences are not obstacles but opportunities for growth if approached with empathy and curiosity. I also realized that effective communication requires adaptability and a willingness to step outside one’s own cultural framework.

As a student and future professional in Computer Science, I plan to promote understanding and empathy by integrating cultural awareness into technology design and development. A concrete action I can take is to prioritize inclusive design practices that consider the diverse needs and perspectives of users from various cultural backgrounds. For example, I will actively research and incorporate cultural variation into user interfaces, ensuring that language, symbols, and functionality are accessible and respectful across different cultures. Additionally, I will advocate for diversity and inclusion initiatives in tech teams, pushing for representation that reflects the global user base of technology products.
