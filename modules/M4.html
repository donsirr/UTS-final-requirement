How do you view and appreciate your physical self, and what personal commitments can you make to care for and respect your body?
My view of my physical self has evolved significantly over the years, shifting from a place of self-criticism to one of appreciation and respect. Growing up, I often compared myself to others, focusing on what I perceived as flaws rather than strengths. I remember feeling frustrated with my lack of athleticism in high school, or wishing I could change certain features to fit an ideal I’d seen in media or among peers. It wasn’t until college, when I began to prioritize my well-being over external expectations, that my perspective started to change.

Now, I see my body not as an object to be perfected, but as a tool and a partner—one that allows me to experience the world, pursue my passions, and connect with others. I’ve come to appreciate its resilience and capability. Whether it’s staying up late to debug a piece of code, or simply carrying me through a busy day, my body has proven itself to be remarkably adaptable. I’m grateful for the ways it enables me to engage with life—my hands for typing and creating, my legs for exploring, and my senses for savoring art, nature, and food.

That said, I’ve not always treated my body with the care it deserves. In the past, I’ve prioritized productivity over rest, skipped meals during busy periods, and neglected physical activity when stressed. These habits have taken a toll, leaving me feeling drained and disconnected from my body. I recall a particularly intense semester when I pulled multiple all-nighters, subsisted on caffeine and snacks, and ignored my body’s pleas for rest. By the end, I felt physically and emotionally depleted, a stark reminder that my body is not a machine but a living entity that requires nurturing.

Moving forward, I’m committed to honoring my physical self with intentional care and respect. This means prioritizing sleep, not as a luxury but as a necessity, recognizing that rest is essential for both my physical and mental health. It means moving my body regularly, whether through jogging, or a simple walk, not to change its appearance but to celebrate its strength and vitality. It means eating mindfully, focusing on nourishing my body with balanced meals and listening to its hunger cues, rather than treating food as an afterthought.

I also want to practice gratitude for my body, taking a moment each day to appreciate what it allows me to do, rather than fixating on how it looks. This shift in mindset is crucial—it’s about valuing function over form, resilience over perfection. Lastly, I’m committed to listening to my body, paying attention to its signals of fatigue or stress, and responding with rest or self-care, rather than pushing through.

By embracing these practices, I hope to cultivate a relationship with my physical self that is rooted in respect, gratitude, and care. After all, my body is not just a vessel for my ambitions but a vital part of who I am—and it deserves to be treated as such.
How do you understand your sexuality, sexual orientation, and gender identity, and how do these aspects shape your sense of self?
My understanding of my sexuality, sexual orientation, and gender identity has been a deeply personal and evolving journey, one that has significantly shaped my sense of self. Growing up, I never questioned my sexual orientation—it felt innate and straightforward. However, as I’ve grown older and more self-aware, I’ve begun to appreciate the nuances of sexuality and gender beyond binary categories. I’ve learned to respect and admire the diversity of human experience, recognizing that everyone’s journey is unique. This awareness has made me more empathetic and open-minded, both in how I view others and how I understand myself.

My sexuality and gender identity have influenced my sense of self in several ways. As a heterosexual man, I’ve often felt pressure to conform to traditional masculine norms—being strong, assertive, and emotionally stoic. While some of these traits come naturally to me, others feel restrictive. For example, I’ve had to unlearn the idea that showing vulnerability or seeking emotional support is a sign of weakness. Instead, I’ve come to see these acts as essential to my well-being and authenticity.

At the same time, my identity has also given me a sense of belonging and connection. It’s allowed me to form deep, meaningful relationships with others, both romantic and platonic, and to find community with people who share similar experiences. These connections have been a source of joy, support, and personal growth.

However, I’m also aware of the privilege that comes with my identity. As a man, I’ve never had to face the discrimination or challenges that many LGBTQ+ individuals endure. This awareness has deepened my commitment to being an ally, advocating for equality, and creating spaces where everyone feels seen and valued, regardless of their sexual orientation or gender identity.

Ultimately, my sexuality and gender identity are integral to who I am, but they don’t define me entirely. They are threads in the tapestry of my identity, woven together with my values, experiences, and relationships. Embracing these aspects of myself has allowed me to live more authentically, with greater empathy and understanding for both myself and others.
What are your thoughts and personal stands on the issues of body shaming, gender-based violence, and discrimination? How do these issues affect individuals and society as a whole?
The issues of body shaming, gender-based violence, and discrimination are deeply troubling and reflect broader societal problems that affect individuals and communities on a profound level. Personally, I stand firmly against these injustices, believing that every person deserves to be treated with dignity, respect, and equality, regardless of their body type, gender, or identity.

Body shaming is something I find particularly harmful because it attacks individuals at their core, often targeting aspects of their appearance that are beyond their control. I’ve seen how comments about weight, skin color, or physical features can erode a person’s self-esteem and mental health. It’s a form of bullying that perpetuates unrealistic beauty standards and fosters a culture of comparison and self-criticism. I believe everyone has the right to feel comfortable in their own skin, and body shaming undermines that fundamental right. It’s a reminder that words have power, and we must use them to uplift, not to tear down.

Gender-based violence is a pervasive issue that disproportionately affects women, non-binary individuals, and other marginalized groups. Whether it’s physical, emotional, or sexual abuse, this violence is a stark manifestation of power imbalances and toxic gender norms. I’ve witnessed how it can trap individuals in cycles of fear and trauma, limiting their ability to live freely and fully. On a societal level, it perpetuates inequality, normalizes aggression, and undermines the safety and well-being of entire communities. I believe it’s everyone’s responsibility to challenge the attitudes and systems that enable this violence, and to support survivors with compassion and action.

Discrimination, in all its forms, is a barrier to justice and human flourishing. Whether based on gender, race, sexuality, or ability, discrimination creates divisions and denies people opportunities to thrive. I’ve seen how it can exclude individuals from education, employment, and social spaces, reinforcing cycles of poverty and marginalization. It’s not just an individual issue but a systemic one, rooted in historical biases and institutional practices. I believe we must actively work to dismantle these systems, advocating for policies and cultures that promote inclusivity and equity.

These issues are interconnected, each reinforcing the other in a cycle of harm. Body shaming often targets those already marginalized by gender or racial discrimination, while gender-based violence is frequently enabled by discriminatory attitudes and practices. Together, they create a society where many people feel unsafe, undervalued, and unseen.

On a personal level, I’m committed to being part of the solution. This means educating myself about these issues, speaking out against injustice when I see it, and supporting organizations that work to create change. It also means examining my own biases and behaviors, ensuring that I’m not inadvertently contributing to the problem. I believe that by standing against body shaming, gender-based violence, and discrimination, we can move toward a more just and compassionate society—one where everyone has the opportunity to live with dignity and respect.
